\section{Introduction}
Streamflow prediction plays an important role in water resource management, flood control and disaster mitigation, as well as basin operation and regulation\cite{GAO2026103148}. In recent years, machine learning and deep learning models have achieved significant performance improvements in streamflow prediction tasks, demonstrating strong capability in capturing complex nonlinear relationships and temporal features\cite{apak2026multi, ZIPPER2026134909}. However, in practical hydrological research and decision-making applications, achieving high predictive accuracy alone is often insufficient. Many high-performance models still behave as “black boxes\cite{Núñez2023_XAI},” whose internal decision-making mechanisms are difficult to interpret intuitively, leading to limited transparency and reliability of the prediction results\cite{WangShap2024}. Therefore, how to reveal the decision basis of models while maintaining predictive performance, and to understand the influence of different input variables on prediction outcomes, has become a key issue in streamflow prediction research\cite{MardShap2022}.

To mitigate the "black-box" nature of data-driven models, researchers have increasingly integrated hydrological process knowledge into neural networks to enhance physical consistency\cite{SCHMID2026124819}. Notably, Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) embed governing physical equations directly into the loss function as penalty terms, ensuring that the training process adheres to both observational data and fundamental physical laws\cite{SHI2025_PINN}. This approach bolsters the model's generalization capability and physical plausibility to a significant extent\cite{LIU2026_PINN}. For instance, Fang et al.\cite{FANG2025_PINN} developed the SWAN model (a PINN variant) by directly embedding saturated runoff mechanisms into the neural network architecture. This paradigm achieves the high predictive accuracy characteristic of deep learning while providing physical grounding for internal states like soil water storage.

However, physical consistency does not inherently equate to model interpretability\hskip 0pt plus 1pt\cite{Roscher2020_Explainable}. Consequently, even with physical knowledge embedded, the internal decision-making logic of the network remains largely opaque\cite{Krishnapriyan2021_PINN}, making it difficult to quantify the specific contributions of individual input variables to streamflow prediction\cite{NASER2026_Inrter}. To bridge this gap, model-agnostic post-hoc interpretability techniques, such as SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP)\cite{lundberg2017_Shap_Origin} and Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME)\cite{Ribeiro2016_LIME_Origin}, have gained prominence. These methods facilitate a systematic evaluation of feature importance, offering transparent insights into the predictive mechanics of otherwise "black-box" hydrological neural networks\cite{Clark2025_Shap,Wagan2026_LIME}.

LIME was an early post-hoc method that explains individual predictions by approximating a black-box model locally with a simple surrogate\cite{Nagara2022_LIME}. While effective for local interpretability, LIME struggles to capture global model behavior, and its output suffers from unclear mathematical logic that hinders intuitive interpretation\cite{dieber2020_LIME_problem}. To overcome these limitations, SHAP was developed based on cooperative game theory, assigning consistent and theoretically grounded contribution scores to each feature\cite{ZHANG2026_SHAP}. SHAP can provide both local and global insights, making it particularly suitable for interpreting complex models like neural networks in hydrological applications\cite{LU2026125531}.

Despite its theoretical appeal, SHAP has several inherent limitations that can affect the reliability of its explanations\cite{kumar2020_Shap_problem}. First, there exists an accuracy-reliability gap: even if a model achieves high predictive performance, such as a large $R^2$, the feature attributions provided by SHAP may not reflect the true hydrological mechanisms\cite{TAKEFUJI2026_Shap_problem}. Accurate predictions can result from spurious correlations or complex mathematical fitting\cite{e24050687_Shap_problem}, without capturing the underlying interactions among rainfall, soil moisture, evapotranspiration, and other hydrological processes\cite{Benchmarkingofa_PhysicallyBasedHydrologicModel}. Moreover, unlike predictions that can be validated against observed streamflow, feature importance lacks an objective ground truth, making SHAP explanations difficult to independently verify\cite{NEURIPS2021_shap_residuals}. Second, SHAP assumes feature independence, which is often violated in hydrological systems where input variables are highly interdependent\cite{HUANG2024_shap_problems,w11050905_related}. For instance, rainfall, upstream flow, soil saturation, and land use factors interact nonlinearly to influence streamflow, so SHAP may produce misleading attributions and fail to identify the true drivers of hydrological variability\cite{TAKEFUJI2025123514}.

Current explainability methods for deep learning-based streamflow prediction generally share the following limitations: \textbf{(1)  Limited verifiability:} most methods lack clear quantitative metrics, making it difficult to systematically validate the reliability of their explanations; \textbf{(2) Neglect of input variable dependencies:} under highly correlated hydrological inputs, traditional methods often produce explanations that deviate from the true causal relationships; \textbf{(3) Structural-level constraints:} existing approaches are typically based on statistical assumptions or additional restrictions, providing only local feature contributions or single-layer analyses, without truly examining the internal model structure. As a result, they fail to offer end-to-end explanations from inputs to internal nodes to outputs, and cannot reveal key nodes and their interactions.

To address these limitations, this study proposes a novel explainability method, Sparse Transformer-based Explainable Hydrology (STEH), which leverages the Sparse Transformer paradigm to achieve full-chain explainability from inputs, through model structure, to predicted outputs. Specifically: \textbf{(1) Learnable gates for nodes and edges:} learnable gating mechanisms are introduced for input features, attention heads, and neurons. Sparse training automatically extracts minimal critical sub-circuits, reducing redundant structures and facilitating subsequent analysis; \textbf{(2) Internal ablation validation:} by performing ablation experiments on individual input nodes, attention heads, and neurons, the actual contribution of each element to the prediction is quantified, providing verifiable metrics and enabling identification of core network units, whhic is a structural proxies of physical process; \textbf{(3) Structural interactions and physical analysis:} bridges are constructed between input variables and internal nodes, evaluating structural dependency and information flow based on core network, while linear probes are used to assess the physical interpretability of hidden states; \textbf{(4) Physical model validation:} the Hydrologiska Fyrans Vattenbalans (HBV) model is refined based on the explainability analysis, validating the effectiveness of the findings in real hydrological simulations.

This method addresses the current shortcomings of explainability approaches, including limited verifiability, neglect of input correlations, and incomplete coverage of internal node interactions. It provides a quantitative basis for understanding whether deep learning models truly learn hydrological processes, and offers preliminary insights into the key question: “\emph{Are Deep Learning Models Learning Hydrological Processes?}”
